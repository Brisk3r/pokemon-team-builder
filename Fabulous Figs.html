The purpose of this website is to fulfil my school assignment requirements while exploring the incredible benefits of whole foods. Why Eat a Wide Range of Fruits and Vegetables?

Including a colourful, wide variety of fruits and vegetables in our diet is crucial for maintaining good health. Different plant foods provide different combinations of essential vitamins, minerals, dietary fibre, and antioxidants. Eating a diverse range helps protect the body against chronic diseases, supports a strong immune system, and ensures we get all the micro-nutrients needed to achieve our wellness goals.

Recommended Daily Intake

According to the Australian Dietary Guidelines, to support healthy growth and development, the recommended daily intake for children and adolescents (roughly ages 9-18) is:

  • Fruit: 2 serves per day
  • Vegetables: 5 to 5.5 serves per day

The Importance of Breakfast

Breakfast is often considered the most important meal of the day because it breaks the overnight fasting period. It replenishes your supply of glucose to boost energy levels and alertness, while also providing other essential nutrients required for good health. A healthy breakfast improves concentration, performance at school, and sets a positive foundation for making healthy choices throughout the rest of the day.

Page 1: The Nutrition of a Fig

Identify the Fruit

The focus food for this assignment is the Fig (scientific name: Ficus carica). Known for its unique, sweet taste and soft, chewy texture, it is a fantastic, versatile fruit.

Health Benefits

Figs are an excellent source of dietary fibre, which promotes a healthy digestive system and helps keep you feeling fuller for longer. They are rich in antioxidants, particularly in their skin, which help fight free radicals in the body. Additionally, their potassium content can assist in maintaining healthy blood pressure levels.

Nutritional Value of the Whole Food

  • Carbohydrates: Figs are primarily made of carbohydrates, predominantly in the form of natural sugars (glucose and fructose) which provide excellent, readily available energy. They are also rich in complex carbohydrates in the form of dietary fibre.
  • Protein: They contain a very small amount of protein, which is typical for most fruits. They should be paired with nuts, dairy, or meats for a protein-complete meal.
  • Lipids (Fats): Fresh figs are naturally virtually fat-free, making them a very lean addition to the diet.
  • Vitamins & Minerals: Packed with essential micronutrients including Vitamin K (important for blood clotting), Vitamin B6 (helps the brain and nervous system), potassium, calcium, and magnesium.
  • Water: Fresh figs have a high water content (roughly 80%), which helps with daily hydration and makes them a refreshing snack.

Page 2: Eating Figs

Varieties Available

There are several common varieties of figs you can find in stores or at local markets:

  • Black Genoa: Large size with dark purple skin and sweet red flesh.
  • Brown Turkey: Brownish-purple skin with a milder, less sweet flavour.
  • White Adriatic: Pale green skin with a very sweet, strawberry-coloured centre.

How to Prepare

Figs are highly versatile and easy to prepare. First, wash them gently under cold water, as they are very delicate. You can eat them whole—the skin is completely edible! If you prefer, you can peel them. They can be sliced to top dishes, lightly baked to caramelise their natural sugars, or even grilled to add a smoky flavour.

Incorporating Figs into Your Diet

Breakfast

Slice fresh figs over your morning porridge, yogurt, or cereal for natural sweetness and extra fibre.

Lunch

Toss quartered figs into a leafy green salad with a light vinaigrette and some feta or goat cheese.

Snacks & Baking

Eat them whole as a quick, energising snack, or bake them into healthy homemade muffins and breads.

Warm Fig & Honey Oatmeal

Breakfast

Ingredients (Serves 1):

  • 1/2 cup rolled oats
  • 1 cup milk (or almond milk)
  • 2 fresh figs, chopped
  • 1 tsp honey
  • 1 tbsp crushed walnuts
  • Pinch of cinnamon

Method:

  1. Combine oats and milk in a saucepan over medium heat.
  2. Simmer for 5-7 minutes until creamy, stirring occasionally.
  3. Stir in half the chopped figs and the cinnamon.
  4. Pour into a bowl, top with the remaining fresh figs, walnuts, and a drizzle of honey.

Why I chose this: I chose this for breakfast because oats provide long-lasting, slow-release energy, while the figs add a wonderful natural sweetness and an extra boost of digestive fibre to start the day right for school.

Fig, Rocket & Feta Salad

Lunch

Ingredients (Serves 2):

  • 3 cups fresh rocket (arugula) leaves
  • 4 fresh figs, quartered
  • 50g reduced-fat feta cheese, crumbled
  • 1/4 cup sliced almonds, toasted
  • 1 tbsp olive oil
  • 1 tbsp balsamic vinegar

Method:

  1. Wash and dry the rocket leaves, placing them in a large serving bowl.
  2. Gently scatter the quartered figs and crumbled feta over the leaves.
  3. Sprinkle the toasted almonds on top.
  4. In a small jar, shake together the olive oil and balsamic vinegar, then dress the salad just before serving.

Why I chose this: I chose this as a lunch recipe because it is incredibly quick to prepare, light on the stomach, and provides a beautiful contrast between the sweet, soft figs and the peppery rocket. It won't leave you feeling sluggish in the afternoon.

Balsamic Baked Chicken with Roasted Figs

Dinner

Ingredients (Serves 4):

  • 4 lean chicken breasts
  • 8 fresh figs, halved
  • 2 sprigs fresh rosemary
  • 2 tbsp balsamic glaze
  • 1 tbsp olive oil
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Steamed green beans (to serve)

Method:

  1. Preheat oven to 200°C. Place chicken breasts in a baking dish.
  2. Rub chicken with olive oil, salt, pepper, and rosemary leaves.
  3. Arrange the halved figs around the chicken in the dish.
  4. Drizzle everything with balsamic glaze.
  5. Bake for 25-30 minutes until chicken is cooked through and figs are soft and caramelised. Serve with steamed green beans.

Why I chose this: Dinner needs to be protein-rich to help muscle recovery after a busy day or playing sports. Baking the figs makes them act almost like a sweet, rich sauce for the lean chicken breast. It feels like a premium, wholesome meal that is easy to cook for the whole family.
